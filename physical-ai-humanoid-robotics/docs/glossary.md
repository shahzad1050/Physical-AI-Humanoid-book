# Glossary of Terms: Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics

This glossary provides definitions for key terms used throughout the Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics textbook.

## A

**Actuator**: A component responsible for moving or controlling a mechanism or system, typically converting electrical, hydraulic, or pneumatic energy into mechanical motion.

**Admittance Control**: A control strategy where the robot's motion is controlled based on applied forces, making the robot behave like a mechanical system with desired admittance properties.

**Affordance**: The possibility of an action that an object provides to an agent; for example, a handle affords grasping.

**AI Perception**: The ability of artificial systems to interpret sensory data to understand and navigate their environment.

**Angular Velocity**: The rate of change of angular displacement of a rotating body, typically measured in radians per second.

## B

**Balance Control**: The process of maintaining a robot's center of mass within its support polygon to prevent falling.

**Behavior Tree**: A hierarchical structure used in robotics and game AI to organize and execute sequences of actions and decisions.

**Bipedal Locomotion**: The act of walking on two legs, a key capability for humanoid robots.

**Bounding Box**: A rectangular box aligned with the coordinate axes that completely encloses an object, used for collision detection and spatial reasoning.

## C

**Center of Mass (CoM)**: The point where the total mass of a body can be considered concentrated for the purpose of analyzing translational motion.

**Center of Pressure (CoP)**: The point where the total sum of pressure forces acts on a surface, particularly important for balance in robotics.

**Closed-loop Control**: A control system that uses feedback to adjust its output based on the difference between the desired and actual state.

**Collision Detection**: The computational problem of detecting when two or more objects come into contact.

**Compliance Control**: A control strategy that allows for controlled flexibility in robot behavior, often used for safe interaction with the environment.

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand visual information from the world.

**Configuration Space (C-space)**: The space of all possible configurations of a robot, where each point represents a valid configuration.

**Control Theory**: An interdisciplinary branch of engineering and mathematics that deals with the behavior of dynamical systems with inputs, and how their behavior is modified by feedback.

**Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)**: A class of deep neural networks commonly applied to analyzing visual imagery.

**Coordinate Frame**: A system for assigning coordinates to points in space, used to describe positions and orientations of objects.

**Coriolis Force**: An apparent force that acts on objects moving within a rotating reference frame.

**Coulomb Friction**: The friction experienced when two dry surfaces slide against each other.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with multiple layers to model and understand complex patterns in data.

**Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) Parameters**: A mathematical convention for attaching coordinate frames to the links of a kinematic chain, used for forward and inverse kinematics.

**Derivative (D) Control**: The component of PID control that responds to the rate of change of the error signal.

**Dexterity**: The measure of a robot's ability to perform fine manipulation tasks, often related to the number and arrangement of degrees of freedom.

**Differential Drive**: A common configuration for wheeled mobile robots where two wheels are independently controlled to achieve steering and motion.

**Dynamics**: The study of forces and torques that cause motion in mechanical systems.

## E

**End Effector**: The device at the end of a robot arm designed to interact with the environment, such as a gripper or tool.

**Episodic Memory**: The part of long-term memory that records personal experiences that are linked with specific times and places.

**Error Signal**: The difference between the desired and actual output in a control system, used to drive corrective action.

**Euclidean Distance**: The "ordinary" straight-line distance between two points in Euclidean space.

**Extrinsic Calibration**: The process of determining the position and orientation of a sensor relative to a robot's coordinate system.

## F

**Feedback Control**: A control system that uses information about the output to adjust the input, creating a closed loop.

**Field of View (FOV)**: The extent of the observable world that is seen at any given moment by a camera or sensor.

**Force Control**: A control strategy that regulates the forces exerted by a robot on its environment.

**Forward Kinematics**: The process of determining the position and orientation of the end effector given the joint angles of a robot.

**Frame Rate**: The frequency at which consecutive images are displayed in a video system, typically measured in frames per second (FPS).

**Friction Cone**: A geometric representation of the limits of static friction forces that can be applied at a contact point.

## G

**Gazebo**: A physics-based 3D simulation environment for robotics research and development.

**Geometric Jacobian**: A matrix that relates joint velocities to end-effector velocities in robotics.

**Grasp Planning**: The process of determining how to grasp an object, including where to place the fingers and how to approach the object.

**Gravity Compensation**: Control strategies that account for gravitational forces to improve robot performance and stability.

**Gripper**: A device attached to the end of a robot arm for grasping and holding objects.

**Ground Truth**: Accurate information about an experimental situation that is gathered by direct observation or measurement, used as a reference for evaluating algorithms.

## H

**Heuristic**: A practical approach to problem-solving that is not guaranteed to be optimal but is sufficient for reaching an immediate goal.

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The field of study focusing on interactions between humans and robots.

**Hyperparameter**: A parameter whose value is set before the learning process begins, as opposed to parameters that are learned from data.

**Hysteresis**: The dependence of the state of a system on its history, commonly observed in friction and magnetic materials.

## I

**Impedance Control**: A control strategy that regulates the dynamic relationship between a robot's motion and the forces it applies to the environment.

**Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)**: An electronic device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

**Integral (I) Control**: The component of PID control that responds to the accumulated error over time.

**Inverse Dynamics**: The calculation of forces and torques required to produce a given motion.

**Inverse Kinematics**: The process of determining joint angles required to achieve a desired position and orientation of the end effector.

**Isaac Sim**: NVIDIA's high-fidelity simulation environment built on Omniverse for robotics and AI development.

**Isaac SDK**: NVIDIA's robotics software development kit for creating AI-powered robotic applications.

## J

**Jacobian Matrix**: A matrix of partial derivatives that describes the local linear relationship between joint space and task space velocities in robotics.

**Joint Limits**: The physical or software-imposed constraints on the range of motion of a robot joint.

**Jerk**: The rate of change of acceleration; the third derivative of position with respect to time.

## K

**Kalman Filter**: An algorithm that uses a series of measurements observed over time to estimate unknown variables, producing estimates that tend to be more accurate than those based on a single measurement alone.

**Kinematics**: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause the motion.

**Kullback-Leibler Divergence**: A measure of how one probability distribution is different from a second, reference probability distribution.

## L

**Lagrangian Mechanics**: A reformulation of classical mechanics that uses the principle of least action.

**Laser Range Finder**: A device that measures distance by illuminating a target with a laser and measuring the time for the reflected light to return.

**Latency**: The time delay between the input and the response in a system.

**Leaky Bucket**: An algorithm used in packet-switched computer networks to mediate data flow.

**Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR)**: A control technique that uses state feedback to minimize a quadratic cost function.

**LIPM (Linear Inverted Pendulum Model)**: A simplified model of bipedal walking that approximates the center of mass as an inverted pendulum.

## M

**Machine Learning**: A method of data analysis that automates analytical model building using algorithms that iteratively learn from data.

**Manipulability**: A measure of how well a robot can apply forces and motions in different directions.

**Mass Matrix**: A matrix that represents the inertial properties of a mechanical system in joint space.

**Matrix Exponential**: A matrix function that generalizes the scalar exponential function to matrices.

**Mean Squared Error (MSE)**: A measure of the quality of an estimatorâ€”it is always non-negative, and values closer to zero are better.

**Monte Carlo Method**: A computational algorithm that relies on repeated random sampling to obtain numerical results.

**MPC (Model Predictive Control)**: An advanced control method that uses a model of the system to predict future behavior and optimize control actions.

## N

**NaN (Not a Number)**: A numeric data type value representing undefined or unrepresentable values.

**Neural Network**: A computing system inspired by the biological neural networks that constitute animal brains.

**Newton-Euler Method**: A recursive algorithm for computing inverse dynamics of serial chain manipulators.

**Null Space**: The set of all vectors that are mapped to the zero vector by a given linear transformation.

## O

**Observability**: A measure of how well internal states of a system can be inferred from knowledge of its external outputs.

**Omnidirectional**: Capable of movement in any direction.

**Operational Space Control**: A control framework that allows specifying desired motion and forces directly in task space rather than joint space.

**Oscillator**: A physical system that exhibits periodic motion.

**Overfitting**: A modeling error that occurs when a function is too closely fitted to a limited set of data points.

## P

**PID Controller**: A control loop feedback mechanism widely used in industrial control systems, consisting of Proportional, Integral, and Derivative terms.

**Particle Filter**: A set of algorithms used to solve the filtering problem in signal processing and statistical inference.

**Path Planning**: The computational process of finding a valid sequence of configurations to move from a start to a goal state.

**Pendulum**: A weight suspended from a pivot so that it can swing freely.

**Perception**: The process of acquiring, interpreting, selecting, and organizing sensory information.

**Pneumatic**: Operated by air or gas under pressure.

**Point Cloud**: A set of data points in space, typically representing the external surface of an object.

**Polar Coordinates**: A two-dimensional coordinate system in which each point is determined by a distance from a reference point and an angle from a reference direction.

**Proportional (P) Control**: The component of PID control that responds to the current error signal.

**Proxemics**: The study of the spatial distances that people maintain in different social contexts.

## Q

**Quaternion**: A number system that extends the complex numbers, commonly used to represent rotations in 3D space.

**Q-Learning**: A model-free reinforcement learning algorithm to learn quality of actions telling an agent what action to take under what circumstances.

## R

**RANSAC (Random Sample Consensus)**: An iterative method to estimate parameters of a mathematical model from a set of observed data that contains outliers.

**Reachability**: The ability of a robot to reach a given point in space with its end effector.

**Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)**: A class of artificial neural networks where connections between nodes form a directed graph along a temporal sequence.

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning where an agent learns to make decisions by performing actions and receiving rewards or penalties.

**Resolution**: The smallest increment an instrument can detect and display.

**Riemannian Metric**: A metric tensor on a Riemannian manifold that defines the distance between points.

**Robot Operating System (ROS)**: Flexible framework for writing robot software that provides services designed for a heterogeneous computer cluster.

**Rolling Friction**: The frictional force that opposes the rolling motion of a body.

**Root Mean Square (RMS)**: A measure of the magnitude of a varying quantity.

**Routh-Hurwitz Criterion**: A mathematical test that establishes the stability of a linear time invariant system.

## S

**Sampling Rate**: The number of samples of a signal taken per second.

**Sensor Fusion**: The combining of sensory data or data derived from disparate sources such that the resulting information has less uncertainty than would be possible when these sources were used individually.

**Servo Motor**: A rotary actuator that allows for precise control of angular position, velocity and acceleration.

**Singularity**: A point where a mathematical object is not defined or behaves badly.

**Sliding Mode Control**: A nonlinear control method that alters the dynamics of a nonlinear system by applying a discontinuous control signal.

**Social Robotics**: The study and development of robots that interact with humans in a socially acceptable manner.

**Spline**: A piecewise-defined polynomial function used for curve fitting and interpolation.

**Stability**: The property of a system to return to equilibrium after a disturbance.

**State Estimation**: The process of estimating the internal state of a dynamic system from measurements.

**Support Polygon**: The convex hull of all points of contact with the ground, crucial for balance in bipedal robots.

**SVD (Singular Value Decomposition)**: A factorization of a real or complex matrix that has many useful applications in signal processing and statistics.

## T

**Task Space**: The space in which the robot's end-effector operates, typically defined by position and orientation.

**Temporal Difference Learning**: A prediction-based method in reinforcement learning.

**Tetrahedral Mesh**: A mesh composed of tetrahedra, used in finite element analysis and 3D modeling.

**Thermal Drift**: The tendency of sensor readings to change due to temperature variations.

**Tolerance**: The permissible limit of variation in a physical dimension or property.

**Trajectory**: The path that a moving object follows through space as a function of time.

**Trust Region**: A term used in optimization to denote the subset of the domain of the objective function to be used at the current iteration.

## U

**UKF (Unscented Kalman Filter)**: A variant of the Kalman filter designed for nonlinear systems.

**Underactuated**: A mechanical system with fewer control inputs than degrees of freedom.

**Undershoot**: The transitory values of a system's response before it settles to its final value, specifically referring to values below the steady-state value.

**Unit Quaternion**: A quaternion with magnitude 1, commonly used to represent rotations.

**Unity**: A cross-platform game engine used for creating games, interactive 3D applications, and robotics simulations.

**Unsupervised Learning**: A type of machine learning algorithm used to draw inferences from datasets without labeled responses.

**Urdf (Unified Robot Description Format)**: An XML format for representing robot models in ROS.

## V

**Vanishing Point**: The point in a perspective drawing where parallel lines appear to converge.

**Variance**: A measure of how spread out a distribution is.

**Vector Field**: An assignment of a vector to each point in a subset of space.

**Velocity**: The rate of change of displacement with respect to time; a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction.

**Verlet Integration**: A numerical method used to integrate Newton's equations of motion.

**Vision System**: A system that uses cameras and image processing to perceive and interpret the environment.

## W

**Wavefront Propagation**: A path planning algorithm that propagates wavefronts from the goal to find optimal paths.

**White Noise**: A random signal having equal intensity at different frequencies, giving it a constant power spectral density.

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that coordinates all degrees of freedom of a robot to achieve multiple tasks simultaneously.

**Wrench**: A six-dimensional vector combining forces and torques applied to a rigid body.

**Workspace**: The volume of space that can be reached by the robot's end effector.

## X

**Xacro**: An XML macro language for creating robot descriptions in ROS, extending URDF.

## Y

**Yaw**: The rotation of an object about its vertical axis.

## Z

**Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: A point on the ground where the net moment of the ground reaction force is zero, crucial for bipedal balance.

**Z-buffering**: A method used in 3D computer graphics to determine which objects are visible and which are hidden.

**Z-transform**: A mathematical tool used in signal processing to analyze discrete-time signals and systems.
